4 Managing "Instrument Parameters"

Now that you understand have configured your default studio to suit your own needs, you might be wondering just how to make use of all those programs and controllers you set up. As I have mentioned in various other places, program changes and controllers in MIDI work on a channel level. That means they work on a track level within Rosegarden. On the audio side of the aisle, Rosegarden divides audio support into 16 channels as well, and channel-specific settings such as volume levels and plugins can be configured at the track and channel level as well.

4.1 JACK Track or MIDI Track?

When starting with a new track, or reconfiguring an existing one, the first decision you need to make is whether the track will be used for audio or MIDI. Regardless of whether you are displaying connections or track labels, you begin by clicking the mouse cursor in this area, and holding a moment until a menu appears.

You should be presented with a series of nested menus that allow you to choose the MIDI (or JACK) device and channel for the track. Let's have a look at MIDI first:

By selecting "Roland SC-33 #6 (Piano 1)" from this final list, I assigned this track to the same channel and instrument parameters settings already in use by another track. I could just as easily have assigned it to a channel that had not yet been assigned elsewhere, and could have then used the Instrument Parameters box to select a program change. I will discuss this last idea a bit later.

4.1.1 What Do Those Black Boxes Mean?

The column of small boxes to the left of the channel provide a concise way of showing information about the status of these connections. In the examples above, you might have noticed that they were in several different states.

These boxes convey the following information:

This instrument or device isn't very useful to you right now. It is is not in use by a track, and it is not connected to any active output path.

This instrument or device could provide a valid output path, and all you need to do from here is attach it to a track and begin making use of it. It is connected to an active output path, but it is not being used by any track.

A track or tracks are connected to this device, but this is not currently a valid output path, and the events on any tracks that are connected don't have anywhere to go. This instrument or device is in use by a track, but is not connected to an active output path.

This instrument or device provides a valid output path, and that path is in use by at least one track. It has a valid connection to an output path, and that connection is in use by a track.

In addition, the instrument in use by the current track will have an extra black outline around either a black filled or black outlined box, depending on whether or not the path is connected to a valid output:

The active track is connected to an output that is also connected. If you play this track, you can expect the device you've hooked up to make some noise.

The active track is connected to an output that is not connected. This track, on the other hand, won't make any noise until you assign a different device to it or turn on the device that isn't currently working.

4.3 Creating a JACK Track

If you want to use a track for audio, you'll need to make it a JACK track. The idea is exactly the same as a MIDI track, except you will only have one device to choose from, and it is called "JACK Audio." (If you do not have a "JACK Audio" device available, please ensure that your JACK server is running. If you have problems with this, see "You Don't Know JACK" later on in this guide.)

4.4 JACK Instrument Parameters

Audio tracks have different controls available in the Instrument Parameters box.. They allow you to control the routing of the audio, and to add layers of plugins. There are redundant Mute, Solo and Record toggles, as well as volume sliders for left and right

stereo channels, and a mono/stereo toggle. ( ).

If you wish to assign plugins to the track, you may use one of the "<no plugin>" buttons to summon a dialog similar to this one. These controls will vary considerably from plugin to plugin. I will cover plugins in more depth later on, in "You Don't Know JACK."

5 Recording Basics

There are three different ways to record with Rosegarden. If you come from a MIDI background, the most obvious thing to do is plug in your MIDI keyboard and hit the record button to record a MIDI segment in the currently-selected track. If audio is your aim, you'll want to set your mixer recording source and get to work. On the other hand, if you have all the keyboard skill of a paraplegic chipmunk, like I do, you might be more interested in step recording.

I will cover step recording later on. For now my focus is on recording operations that can be performed from the main window.
