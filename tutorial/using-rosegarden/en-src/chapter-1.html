Introduction

What is Rosegarden?

Rosegarden-4, hereafter referred to as simply Rosegarden, is a versatile application that seeks to be a useful tool for those wanting to create music. It combines aspects of a MIDI sequencer, an audio sequencer, and a notation editor into one convenient, easy to use package that provides users with a consistent and intuitive interface.

Rosegarden is one of many audio and MIDI applications and utilities for Linux, and it integrates well with related applications. This relationship centers around two different Linux standards. One the one hand, Rosegarden is closely tied to the Advanced Linux Sound Architecture (ALSA) MIDI infrastructure, and it works very well with other ALSA MIDI applications such as Qsynth, ZynAddSubFX and Hydrogen. On the other hand Rosegarden is also tied to the Jack Audio Connection Kit (JACK), and it works well with other JACK applications. Rosegarden can stand well on its own, but its true strength is in how well it combines with other applications as part of a total home studio solution.

About This Guide

This guide is part manual, part tutorial. I attempt to showcase Rosegarden's most significant features, and give you suggestions for how to use (or work around) these features in order to achieve your musical goals.

This guide is generally arranged to be read from cover to cover, and to proceed through Rosegarden's feature set window by window in a logical way. If you wish to home straight in on a specific topic, the table of contents is thorough, and the web version is fully hyperlinked.

Each chapter is loosely divided into two parts. The first half details the mechanical side of making use of various controls in order to manipulate data in some way. The latter half focuses on demonstrating how to use these controls to achieve common musical goals.

Menu options and references to some dialog pages or tabs are formatted in bold, with arrows indicating the progression through the layers of menus. For example, if I say to use File -> Import -> Import MIDI File this means to begin at the "File" menu, find "Import" on this menu and hover on it until the submenu opens out, then click on "Import MIDI File."

Various personal tips and "unofficial" workarounds are scattered throughout, set off in italics.

Version and System Requirements

This document describes Rosegarden4, the latest in this venerable line of applications; not to be confused with the older Rosegarden that was last updated in 1997.

The new Rosegarden is a native KDE application that can run on any system that has the KDE and QT libraries installed, and currently requires a minimum of KDE/QT 3.1 in order to run. As of version 1.0, Rosegarden is closely tied to ALSA, and ALSA has not, to my knowledge, been ported to platforms like OS-X and FreeBSD. Until such porting occurs, the majority of Rosegarden's features are limited to the Linux platform exclusively. It should compile and run on other platforms, but most of its features will be disabled.

Linux Version

The more recent your distribution, the better. Rosegarden and many related, interdependent applications are all very new. We at Rosegarden suggest that you run the very latest release version of the distribution of your choice, at a minimum. We further suggest that a distribution with special emphasis on Linux audio, such as AGNULA or Planet CCRMA might well be worth your consideration if you find yourself having trouble getting all of these things to cooperate. Linux audio is a bit of an arcane and complex area at the present, and these special distributions seek to remove much of the sting. My personal recommendation would be to take a look at DeMuDi, AGNULA's Debian-based live CD distribution. As a matter of personal preference, I try to stay as far away from Red Hat-based distros as possible, and I strongly prefer the Debian package management system. À chaq'un son goût.

Minimum Computer Specs

If you wish to do MIDI only, and if you have a soundcard or external synthesizer capable of doing the work of playing music largely on its own, you can probably get by with as little as PII-233. The more RAM the better.

If you need to use a soft synth such as QSynth for MIDI production, I have found that an 800 MHz machine with 128 MB of RAM is the minimum spec box that I could get working acceptably. This machine was completely useless when using JACK, so I had to run Rosegarden with its audio features disabled, and I had to play QSynth through the ALSA output, rather than the more usual JACK output. (I will cover these topics in detail in "You Don't Know JACK," so don't despair if you have no idea what I'm talking about.)

To get the most out of Rosegarden with the least amount of kernel hacking and other tweaking, I've found a 2.0 GHz P4 with 512 MB of RAM delivers acceptable MIDI and audio performance using a standard, unpatched 2.4 kernel, running both JACK and Rosegarden with normal user privileges, using a cheap Wal-Mart Sound Blaster Live!.

Suggested Minimum Hardware

My recommended minimum soundcard is an emu101k-based card such as the venerable Sound Blaster Live! and its many descendants and clones. This card can do in hardware most of what QSynth can do with software, so you have a decent internal synth for MIDI performance without the CPU penalties. You can still run QSynth if you want (CPU performance allowing) to take advantage of its integrated LADSPA plugins for effects. It has a joystick port MIDI adapter (additional Y-cable or interface box required) for interfacing with external MIDI devices, and it can just barely be coaxed into functioning with JACK without resorting to kernel hacking and arcane audio performance tweaking. A $15 SB Live! and a $50 Wal-Mart home theater system make for a rather decent sounding home audio solution on the cheap.

For real performance, you will probably want to look at professional level soundcards. The mAudio Audiophile 2426 comes highly recommended as a good entry level card. It has a MIDI interface, but only sports a limited number of inputs and outputs. Serious audio people will probably want to look beyond the Audiophile and get into serious money and performance territory. Better solutions offer more inputs and outputs, and nice extras such as breakout boxes, but many do not offer MIDI ports, so you're stuck buying additional hardware if you want to use external MIDI hardware.

In writing this document, I assume that most of my readers are weekend jammers like me, trying to make the best of low quality hardware, and are too cheap or too broke to buy something better. I focus on the SB Live! as the lowest common denominator, because dealing with that card has the greatest chance of being useful to the largest number of people, and because it's a decent, if inferior, all-in-one solution as the centerpiece of a home studio. If you are attempting to use something even more pathetic, then I hope perhaps you can pop for $15 to upgrade to a slightly less crappy soundcard.

If someone wants to buy me some pro gear, I'll be happy to change my perspective and focus on how to get the most out of the big dollar toys. :)
