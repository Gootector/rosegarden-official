How to get involved in Rosegarden Development

We sometimes receive offers for help, and we really like that because it's quite nice to see other people being interested in your work. However, for some reason it seems people don't always know how to become involved. This document attemps to help volunteers on that way. Some things in here might offend you or put you off. Don't be. We're just being pragmatic.

What should I not do ?

  • Emailing us asking for permission to help, or offering help, expecting us to guide you or point you to something to do.

    • We simply don't have the time or skills to manage people whom we don't know. If you want to help, you'll have to be autonomous.

  • Detailing your skills, or even worse, sending us a CV

    • You're not applying for a job here. We're not going to turn any offer of help down. You wanna help ? Great, consider yourself hired. Now get off your butt and go to the next section.

  • Send bright ideas of cool features

    • The overwhelming probability is that, we won't care. Really. The reason is simply that there are already so many things to do before this can be considered a working and useable application than we'll be strongly resisting any "fancy stuff". If you want to code it though, then that's another matter (see below).

To sum up, it's been our experience that this description of the volunteering process is quite true. Please take a look at it, it's a short but very worthwhile read. (note: by "our experience" we don't just mean the last few months after releasing 0.1 - we've all been involved at various levels in free software projects for several years.)

What should I do ?

Just one thing : Send us patches

It's a simple empiric rule : the longer you talk about your contribution, the less likely it is to happen.

People who come up with a patch attached to their first mail almost always end up being regular or at least worthwhile contributors. On the opposite, in the last few months we've already had half a dozen of people who offered help, discussed on stuff, sometimes at great length, and finally just disappeared.

So just go ahead, if you think there's something interesting to do on our code, do it. If you've found a bug and want to work on it, do so (in both cases, be sure to work on the CVS version, though).

This has of course to be balanced by the estimated importance of your contribution. If you plan to start something really big, it's probably better to ask us first, because it will probably interfere with our own development plans. But if it's a neat, self-contained feature, then just go ahead. We'll certainly be glad to integrate it.

Where do I start ?

Subscribe to the rosegarden-devel-admin@ mailing list.

As usual, documentation is too scarce, but there are some design documents in the docs/ directory of the source tree. You can also browse some automatically-generated code reference documentation. If you're stuck somewhere or don't understand something, just ask on the mailing-list.

But I can't code...

If you're a good writer, you can start the user manual. If you're good at drawing, you can draw icons. If you have experience with other sequencers (Logic, Cubase, Cakewalk), you can guide us and answer some of our questions on which UI is best. If you're not any of this, well, it's better if you get some programming skills elsewhere and come back. Rosegarden's code is fairly complex, and not a good place to learn C++ or even Qt/KDE programming.

What we don't need at the moment are, as surprising as it seems, testers. People just reporting bugs. We use the app often enough to notice bugs pretty quickly, and we know there are many. So reporting them without a correction attached is like stating the obvious. As said above, however, reporting and correcting them is extremely welcome.
